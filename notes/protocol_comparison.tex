\section*{Framework}
%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection*{General qubit sensor}

Consider a qubit sensor whose pulse sequence is parametrised by a detuning
$\delta$ from the target frequency.
After the sequence, the qubit Bloch vector is $\vb{r}(\delta)$ and the noise
susceptibility is characterised by the filter function $\vb{F}(\omega;\delta)$.

A single-shot frequency estimate from measuring observable
$\hat{B} = \vb{B}\cdot\boldsymbol{\sigma}$ has variance
\begin{equation}\label{eq:variance}
    \sigma^2_{\rm freq}(\delta)
    = \frac{\chivar_B(\delta)}{\abs{\vb{B}\cdot\dot{\vb{r}}(\delta)}^2},
\end{equation}
where $\dot{\vb{r}} = \partial_\delta\vb{r}$ is the signal slope and the
noise variance in the numerator is given by the Kubo linear-response formula
\begin{equation}\label{eq:chi}
    \chivar_B(\delta)
    = \frac{2}{\pi}\int_0^\infty S(\omega)
      \Bigl[\abs{\vb{F}(\omega;\delta)}^2
            - \abs{\tilde{\vb{B}}\cdot\vb{F}(\omega;\delta)}^2\Bigr]d\omega,
\end{equation}
with $\tilde{\vb{B}} = R^{\mathsf{T}}(\delta)\,\vb{B}$ the measurement axis
rotated into the toggling frame and $S(\omega)$ the one-sided noise power
spectral density.

\subsection*{Cramér--Rao optimal measurement direction}

Minimising~\eqref{eq:variance} over $\vb{B}$ is achieved by choosing
$\vb{B}_{\rm opt} = \dot{\vb{r}}/\abs{\dot{\vb{r}}}$, which gives the
single-shot Cramér--Rao bound
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cr-variance}
    \sigma^2_{\rm CR}(\delta)
    = \frac{\chivar_{\vb{B}_{\rm opt}}(\delta)}{\abs{\dot{\vb{r}}(\delta)}^2}.
\end{equation}

\subsection*{Sensitivity--noise tradeoff and detuning optimisation}

Equation~\eqref{eq:cr-variance} reveals a fundamental tradeoff: $\delta$
simultaneously controls the signal slope $\abs{\dot{\vb{r}}}$ and the noise
susceptibility $\chivar$.  The optimal operating point is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dopt}
    \dopt = \arg\min_\delta\;\sigma^2_{\rm CR}(\delta).
\end{equation}
In general $\dopt \neq 0$: operating off-resonance improves the figure of merit
when the gain in noise rejection outweighs the loss in signal sensitivity.
We define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fomdef}
    \mathrm{FOM}(\delta)
    = \frac{\abs{\dot{\vb{r}}(\delta)}^2}{\chivar_{\vb{B}_{\rm opt}}(\delta)}
    = \frac{1}{\sigma^2_{\rm CR}(\delta)},
\end{equation}
so $\dopt$ is equally the maximiser of the FOM.

\subsection*{Three-level Lambda clock}

We study a Lambda system with levels $\lvert g\rangle$ (ground),
$\lvert e\rangle$ (probe-excited), and $\lvert m\rangle$ (metastable clock
state).  The QSP pulse sequence acts on the $\{g, e\}$ probe transition; the
signal is read from the $\{g, m\}$ clock transition.
All protocols use total interrogation time $T = 2\pi$ (with $\hbar = 1$,
frequencies in rad\,s$^{-1}$).

\paragraph{Laser detuning Hamiltonian.}
The physically correct Hamiltonian for a laser detuned by $\delta$ from the
$g$--$e$ resonance is
\begin{equation}
    H_\delta = \delta\,\lvert e\rangle\langle e\rvert.
    \label{eq:hamiltonian}
\end{equation}
This is \emph{not} traceless in the probe subspace: it shifts only
$\lvert e\rangle$, leaving $\lvert g\rangle$ and $\lvert m\rangle$ unchanged.
Under a continuous drive at Rabi frequency $\Omega$ and detuning $\delta$ for
duration $\tau$, the three-level propagator is
\begin{equation}
    U(\tau) = e^{-i\delta\tau/2}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        f & ig & 0 \\
        ig^* & f^* & 0 \\
        0 & 0 & e^{i\delta\tau/2}
    \end{pmatrix},
    \label{eq:propagator}
\end{equation}
where $f$, $g$ are the Cayley--Klein (CK) amplitudes for the SU(2) rotation in
the probe subspace under the traceless Hamiltonian
$\tilde{H} = \tfrac{\delta}{2}(\lvert e\rangle\langle e\rvert -
\lvert g\rangle\langle g\rvert) + \tfrac{\Omega}{2}\vb{n}\cdot\boldsymbol{\sigma}$.
The global probe phase $e^{-i\delta\tau/2}$ accumulates relative to
$\lvert m\rangle$: ignoring it gives incorrect signal predictions for
multi-segment sequences.

\paragraph{State, observable, and clock signal.}
The initial state is
$\lvert\psi_0\rangle = (\lvert g\rangle+\lvert m\rangle)/\sqrt{2}$
and the measurement operator is
$M = \sigma_y^{gm} = -i\lvert g\rangle\langle m\rvert + i\lvert m\rangle\langle g\rvert$.
The clock signal
\begin{equation}
    S(\delta) = \langle\psi_0\rvert U^\dagger(\delta)\,M\,U(\delta)\lvert\psi_0\rangle
    \label{eq:signal}
\end{equation}
is the $\hat{y}$-component of the $\{g,m\}$ clock-transition Bloch vector,
i.e.\ $S = \vb{B}\cdot\vb{r}(\delta)$ with $\vb{B} = \hat{y}$.
At $\delta = 0$ all protocols considered here have $S(0) = 0$ (zero crossing)
and $\dot{S}(0) \neq 0$, so $\delta = 0$ is the quadrature operating point.

\paragraph{Three-level filter function and $\sigma^2_\nu$.}
The relevant noise variance for frequency estimation is the shot-to-shot
variance of the classical signal mean $\langle M\rangle$:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{V}(\delta)
    = \int_0^\infty \frac{d\omega}{2\pi}\, S(\omega)\, F(\omega;\delta),
    \label{eq:kubo3}
\end{equation}
where the \emph{filter function} for the $\{g,m\}$ observable is
\begin{equation}
    F(\omega;\delta)
    = \abs{\chi(\omega;\delta)}^2,
    \quad
    \chi = \mathcal{F}[\abs{g}^2],
    \label{eq:Fe}
\end{equation}
obtained from the sensitivity trajectory $r(t)=i\abs{g(t)}^2$.
The DC consistency condition $F(0) = \abs{\dot{S}(0)}^2$ ensures the filter
function and sensitivity are not independent.
The normalised frequency variance is
\begin{equation}
    \sigma^2_\nu(0)
    = \frac{1}{\abs{\dot{S}(0)}^2}
      \int_{\omega_{\rm min}}^\infty \frac{d\omega}{2\pi}\, S(\omega)\, F(\omega;0),
    \qquad
    \omega_{\rm min} = \frac{2\pi}{T}.
    \label{eq:fom3}
\end{equation}
The lower cutoff $\omega_{\rm min} = 2\pi/T$ is the Fourier resolution limit:
noise fluctuations slower than one measurement cycle of duration $T$ cannot
be resolved by a single shot and should not contribute to the figure of merit.
This \emph{bandlimited FOM} also removes degeneracy of the optimiser: solutions
with $\chi(t)$ concentrated at $\omega < \omega_{\rm min}$ receive no benefit
from their low noise variance.
We consider two noise PSDs: white noise $S(\omega) = S_0$ and $1/f$ noise
$S(\omega) = S_0/\abs{\omega}$.

\section{Protocol descriptions}
%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Ramsey}

\paragraph{Pulse sequence.}
Two fast $\pi/2$ pulses flanking a free-evolution window:
\begin{equation}
    \underbrace{R_x(\pi/2)}_{\tau_{\pi/2}}
    \;-\;
    \underbrace{\text{free}}_{\tau_{\rm free}}
    \;-\;
    \underbrace{R_x(\pi/2)}_{\tau_{\pi/2}},
    \qquad
    \tau_{\pi/2} = \frac{\pi}{2\Omega_{\rm fast}},
    \quad
    \tau_{\rm free} = T - 2\tau_{\pi/2}.
    \label{eq:ramsey_seq}
\end{equation}
We use $\Omega_{\rm fast} = 20\pi$ so that $\tau_{\pi/2} \ll T$ and the sequence
is effectively a pure free-evolution Ramsey.

\paragraph{Sensitivity at $\delta = 0$.}
The Ramsey sensitivity is
\begin{equation}
    \left.\partial_\delta S\right|_{\delta=0}
    \approx -\frac{T}{2},
    \qquad
    \left|\partial_\delta S\right|^2 \approx \frac{T^2}{4} = \pi^2.
    \label{eq:ramsey_sens}
\end{equation}


\subsection{GPS: Integer Cycle, $m = 1$ and $m = 8$}

\paragraph{Pulse sequence.}
A single continuous Rabi drive on the $\{g,e\}$ probe transition for the full
interrogation time $T$, with Rabi frequency $\Omega = 2\pi m / T$ ($m$ complete
Rabi cycles):
\begin{equation}
    \text{continuous drive at }\Omega = \frac{2\pi m}{T}\text{ for time }T.
    \label{eq:gps_seq}
\end{equation}
For our parameters: $m=1 \Rightarrow \Omega = 1$\,rad\,s$^{-1}$;
$m=8 \Rightarrow \Omega = 8$\,rad\,s$^{-1}$.

\paragraph{Exact clock signal.}
With $H_\delta = \delta\lvert e\rangle\langle e\rvert$ the three-level clock
signal for a single GPS pulse is
\begin{equation}
    S(\delta) = \sin\!\left(\frac{\delta T}{2}\right)\cos\!\left(\frac{\Omega_R T}{2}\right)
              - \frac{\delta}{\Omega_R}\cos\!\left(\frac{\delta T}{2}\right)
                \sin\!\left(\frac{\Omega_R T}{2}\right),
    \qquad
    \Omega_R = \sqrt{\Omega^2 + \delta^2}.
    \label{eq:gps_signal}
\end{equation}
This differs from the probe-subspace-traceless formula $S = (\delta/\Omega_R)\sin(\Omega_R T/2)$;
the two agree only at $\delta = 0$, where both vanish.

\paragraph{Sensitivity at $\delta = 0$.}
Differentiating Eq.~\eqref{eq:gps_signal} and evaluating at $\delta = 0$
(where $\Omega_R = \Omega$):
\begin{equation}
    \left.\partial_\delta S\right|_{\delta=0}
    = \frac{T\,\Omega\,\cos(\Omega T/2) - 2\sin(\Omega T/2)}{2\Omega}.
    \label{eq:gps_deriv}
\end{equation}
For GPS $m$ with $\Omega T = 2\pi m$, we have $\sin(m\pi) = 0$ and
$\cos(m\pi) = (-1)^m$, so
\begin{equation}
    \left.\partial_\delta S\right|_{\delta=0}
    = \frac{(-1)^m\, T}{2},
    \qquad
    \left|\partial_\delta S\right|^2 = \frac{T^2}{4} = \pi^2
    \quad\text{for any integer }m.
    \label{eq:gps_sens}
\end{equation}
All GPS $m$ at $\delta = 0$ therefore have \emph{the same sensitivity as
Ramsey}.  The protocols differ only through their filter functions and the
resulting noise variance $\mathcal{V}$.

\paragraph{Operating point.}
We operate all GPS protocols at $\delta = 0$.  Previous analyses identified
zero-crossings of the old (probe-subspace-traceless) formula as operating points;
those zeros are \emph{not} zeros of the correct signal
Eq.~\eqref{eq:gps_signal}.  For GPS $m=1$, the new signal has no non-trivial
zeros at all.  For GPS $m=8$, non-trivial zeros do exist (e.g.\ at
$\delta \approx 1.008$), but $\sigma^2_\nu$ at those zeros is higher than at $\delta = 0$.


\subsection{Optimal Equiangular $N$-Segment Sequence}

\paragraph{Pulse sequence.}
The sequence is divided into $N$ equal segments of duration $\tau = T/N$.
Each segment is a continuous drive at shared Rabi frequency
$\Omega$ with drive axis $(\cos\phi_k, \sin\phi_k, 0)$:
\begin{equation}
    \prod_{k=1}^{N} \exp\!\left(-i\,\frac{\Omega\tau}{2}\,
    (\cos\phi_k\,\sigma_x + \sin\phi_k\,\sigma_y)\right).
    \label{eq:eq_seq}
\end{equation}
The first phase is fixed to $\phi_1 = 0$ by gauge choice; the free parameters are
$\Omega > 0$ and $\phi_2, \ldots, \phi_N$.

\paragraph{Optimisation.}
We minimise $\sigma^2_\nu = \mathcal{V}(S) / |\partial_\delta S|^2$ over
$(\Omega, \phi_2, \ldots, \phi_N)$ using differential evolution (global search)
followed by L-BFGS-B polishing.
The noise PSD is white ($S(\omega) = S_0$).

\paragraph{Result for $N = 4$.}
The numerical optimum converges to $\Omega T \approx 8.18$ with phases
\begin{equation}
    \phi^* = [0,\;1.476,\;0.154,\;1.178]\ \text{rad},
    \qquad \Omega \approx 1.302\ \text{rad\,s}^{-1}.
    \label{eq:eq_phases}
\end{equation}
Unlike the equiangular-in-phase (CPMG-like) limit $\Omega T \ll 1$, the optimum
uses a non-trivial Rabi frequency that shapes the filter function to reduce the
noise variance while preserving $|\partial_\delta S|^2 \approx \pi^2$.

\subsection*{Observations}

\begin{enumerate}

    \item \textbf{Ramsey: flat, broadband sensitivity.}
          $F_e^{\rm Ramsey}(\omega) \approx T^2/4$ at all frequencies below
          $\sim\!\Omega_{\rm fast}$, with a quadratic roll-off above.

    \item \textbf{GPS $m=1$: $F_e$ concentrated near $\Omega = 1$.}
          The resonant drive at $\Omega = 1$ produces CK amplitudes
          $f(t) = \cos(\Omega t/2)$, $g(t) = -i\sin(\Omega t/2)$.
          The filter function $F_e$ has its main spectral content near
          $\omega = \Omega = 1$ (a peak there, reflecting $|g|^2 \sim \sin^2(\Omega t/2)$)
          and is very small above $\omega \approx 4$.
          Under white noise this concentration near a single low frequency
          gives a noise integral slightly above Ramsey (see Table~\ref{tab:summary}).

    \item \textbf{GPS $m=8$: $F_e$ peaked at $\Omega = 8$ and $2\Omega = 16$.}
          The eight-cycle drive shifts the $F$ peak to $\omega \approx 8$
          and its harmonic at $\omega \approx 16$.  Between $\omega = 1$ and $8$,
          $F$ is moderate, giving a noise integral very close to GPS $m=1$.  The $1/f$ noise $\sigma^2_\nu$ of GPS $m=8$
          is substantially better than GPS $m=1$ because $1/f$ noise weights
          low frequencies heavily, and GPS $m=8$'s $F$ peak sits at $\omega = 8$
          where the $1/f$ weight is eight times smaller than at $\omega = 1$.

    \item \textbf{Equiangular $N=4$: shaped suppression.}
          The optimal phases produce a filter function that sits below Ramsey
          across a broad low-to-mid frequency band while maintaining full
          sensitivity $|\partial_\delta S|^2 \approx \pi^2$.
          The noise integral under white noise is below Ramsey,
          giving the best white-noise $\sigma^2_\nu$ of the four protocols.

\end{enumerate}

\section{Discussion}
%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection*{Effect of correcting the laser-detuning Hamiltonian}

Previous analyses used the probe-subspace-traceless Hamiltonian
$\tilde{H}_\delta = \frac{\delta}{2}(\lvert e\rangle\langle e\rvert -
\lvert g\rangle\langle g\rvert)$, which assigns a \emph{global} probe phase
$e^{-i\delta\tau/2}$ relative to $\lvert m\rangle$ that is absent in the
traceless formula.  This phase is invisible in a two-level analysis but
shifts the signal in the three-level clock.

Under the corrected Hamiltonian (Eq.~\eqref{eq:hamiltonian}), the GPS clock
signal takes the form Eq.~\eqref{eq:gps_signal}, not the simpler formula
$(\delta/\Omega_R)\sin(\Omega_R T/2)$.  As a consequence:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The old zero-crossing operating points $\delta_k = \Omega\sqrt{(k/m)^2 - 1}$
          are \emph{not} zeros of the correct signal.
    \item For GPS $m=1$, the corrected signal has no non-trivial zeros.
    \item For GPS $m=8$, non-trivial zeros exist (e.g.\ $\delta \approx 1.008$) but
          they provide a \emph{lower} FOM than $\delta = 0$ because the drop in
          sensitivity ($|\partial_\delta S|^2 \approx 9.04$) is not compensated
          by a sufficient reduction in noise variance.
    \item At $\delta = 0$, all GPS $m$ recover sensitivity $|\partial_\delta S|^2 = T^2/4$,
          identical to Ramsey (Eq.~\eqref{eq:gps_sens}).
\end{enumerate}
The conclusion: GPS protocols operating at $\delta = 0$ have
\emph{worse} white-noise $\sigma^2_\nu$ than Ramsey, because their filter
functions accumulate more white noise despite equal sensitivity.

\subsection*{White-noise $\sigma^2_\nu$ ordering}

The $\sigma^2_\nu$ ordering under white noise (smaller is better) is:
\[
  \text{Eq. } N{=}4 \;<\; \text{Eq. } N{=}8 \;<\; \text{Ramsey}
  \;\approx\; \text{Eq. } N{=}16 \;<\; \text{GPS }m{=}1 \;\ll\; \text{GPS }m{=}8.
\]
Equiangular $N{=}4$ achieves $\sigma^2_{\nu,\rm white} = 4.02\times10^{-3}$, roughly
$2\times$ better than Ramsey ($8.12\times10^{-3}$), while GPS $m{=}8$ is
$\sim\!6\times$ worse ($4.73\times10^{-2}$) because its chi trajectory oscillates
at $\omega = 8$ and $2\omega = 16$, far above the region of lowest noise weight.
Both GPS protocols have bandlimited noise integrals above Ramsey because the
single continuous drive concentrates $F$ near the Rabi harmonic $\Omega$, which
under flat white noise contributes heavily to $\mathcal{V}$.

\subsection*{$1/f$ noise}

Under $1/f$ noise ($S(\omega) = S_0/|\omega|$), the ordering shifts:
\[
  \text{Eq. } N{=}8 \;<\; \text{Eq. } N{=}4 \;<\; \text{Eq. } N{=}16
  \;\approx\; \text{Ramsey} \;<\; \text{GPS }m{=}8 \;<\; \text{GPS }m{=}1.
\]
GPS $m=8$'s $F$ is concentrated near $\omega = 8$, not near $\omega = 1$.
Under $1/f$ noise the weight $1/\omega$ at $\omega = 8$ is
eight times smaller than at $\omega = 1$, so GPS $m=8$ accumulates an
eight-times-smaller noise contribution per unit $F$ peak.  This is why
GPS $m=8$ ($9.08\times10^{-3}$) outperforms GPS $m=1$ ($8.32\times10^{-3}$)
under $1/f$ noise even though GPS $m=8$ is far \emph{worse} under white noise:
the distinction is \emph{where} their $F$ is non-zero, not its total integral.
Equiangular $N{=}8$ achieves the best $1/f$ performance ($2.37\times10^{-3}$)
by placing chi oscillation in a mid-frequency band that is less exposed to $1/f$
weight than GPS $m=1$ but less penalised by flat white noise than GPS $m=8$.

\subsection*{High-frequency noise}

In atomic clock experiments the local-oscillator (LO) noise spectrum is
typically $1/f$ below $\sim$1\,Hz and white (flat) at higher offset frequencies.
The filter function / Dick-effect framework captures both regimes.

\paragraph{$F_e$ structure of GPS protocols at $\delta = 0$.}
For a resonant continuous drive at Rabi frequency $\Omega$ (i.e.\ $\delta = 0$),
the CK amplitudes are $f(t) = \cos(\Omega t/2)$ and $g(t) = -i\sin(\Omega t/2)$.
The filter function involves $|g(t)|^2 = \sin^2(\Omega t/2)$, which oscillates
at frequency $\Omega$.  Its Fourier transform has a DC component and a
\emph{peak} at $\omega = \Omega$ (and weaker harmonics at $2\Omega, 3\Omega,\ldots$).
This is fundamentally different from a comb of \emph{nulls}: at $\delta = 0$,
GPS protocols have $F_e$ \emph{peaks} at their Rabi harmonics, not zeros.

is shown in Table~\ref{tab:hfnoise} and Fig.~\ref{fig:hf_comparison} (right).

\noindent The dominant feature is GPS $m=1$: its $F_e$ peak at $\omega = 1$ is so
narrow that once $\omega_c > 1$ the remaining Kubo integral is tiny, giving
$\mathrm{FOM}_{1\text{-}{\rm pass}} \sim 10^4$--$10^6$.  This is the
\emph{high-frequency advantage of GPS with a small Rabi frequency}: by
concentrating spectral sensitivity at a single low frequency $\Omega$, any
noise above $\Omega$ is almost entirely rejected.

GPS $m=8$ shows no comparable advantage because its $F_e$ peak is at the
\emph{higher} frequency $\Omega = 8$; noise between $\omega_c = 1$ and $8$ is
still admitted (FOM barely improves from 8.4 to 16.6 as $\omega_c$ goes from
0 to 4).  Only after $\omega_c$ clears both the $\Omega = 8$ and $2\Omega = 16$
peaks does GPS $m=8$'s FOM improve dramatically.

Equiangular $N=4$ sits between: $F_e$ is significantly suppressed above
$\omega \approx 4$, giving $\mathrm{FOM} \sim 10^4$ for $\omega_c = 8$.

\paragraph{Noise concentrated at a specific frequency.}
For a Lorentzian noise peak $S(\omega) \propto [(\omega - \omega_{\rm tech})^2 +
\Gamma^2]^{-1}$, the FOM depends strongly on whether $\omega_{\rm tech}$ falls
near a $F_e$ peak or a zero.  At $\omega_{\rm tech} = 8$ (the GPS $m=8$ Rabi
harmonic), GPS $m=8$ performs the \emph{worst}
($\mathrm{FOM} \approx 3$) because $F_e$ has a peak there.  Equiangular ($\mathrm{FOM} \approx 573$)
and Ramsey ($\mathrm{FOM} \approx 304$) are far superior.

\paragraph{Practical implication.}
The GPS ``integer cycle'' design was originally motivated by nulls in the
\emph{old} (probe-subspace-traceless) filter function at Rabi harmonics.  With
the correct Hamiltonian $H_\delta = \delta|e\rangle\langle e|$ at $\delta = 0$,
these nulls become peaks.  The high-frequency advantage of a GPS-$m$ protocol
is not targeted notching but rather \emph{spectral concentration}: by
choosing $\Omega$ to place the $F_e$ peak well below the noise floor
$\omega_c$, the protocol achieves near-perfect high-frequency rejection.
The optimal choice is the smallest $\Omega$ that still provides sufficient
sensitivity (i.e.\ $|\partial_\delta S|^2 > 0$ at the operating point)—which
for any GPS $m$ at $\delta = 0$ is automatically satisfied with
$|\partial_\delta S|^2 = \pi^2$.
