       Sensitivity, Filtering, and Multilevel Spectroscopy}

\author{Jasmine Sinanan-Singh}
\affiliation{Department of Physics, Institution A}
\author{Leon Zaporski}

\begin{abstract}

Optical atomic clocks require pulse sequences that simultaneously maximize frequency
sensitivity and suppress technical noise---laser phase fluctuations, vibrations,
and magnetic field drifts---within the interrogation bandwidth.
Existing protocols such as Ramsey spectroscopy, composite pulses, and Global Phase
Spectroscopy (GPS) have been analyzed in isolation, with no unified framework for
comparing or optimizing them across different noise environments.
We introduce \emph{temporal Quantum Signal Processing} (temporal QSP), an extension
of canonical QSP to continuous-time interrogation sequences, which maps the
alternating structure of clock pulse sequences onto the polynomial algebra of QSP
and yields closed-form expressions for both the frequency sensitivity and the noise
filter function for any protocol on two-level and three-level $\Lambda$-clock systems.

A central result is a \emph{DC consistency condition} linking the filter function
at zero frequency to the squared signal slope, which shows that sensitivity and
noise suppression are not independently tunable.
This motivates a natural \emph{figure of merit}---the normalized frequency variance
$\sigma^2_\nu$, the ratio of integrated noise power passed by the filter function
to the squared signal slope---that simultaneously captures both dimensions of clock
performance and directly governs the prefactor of the Allan deviation.
Unlike prior filter-function analyses aimed at quantum memory or decoherence
suppression, which optimize noise rejection without regard to signal sensitivity,
the temporal QSP framework keeps both quantities in view.

Within this framework we establish a quantitative performance hierarchy.
Multi-segment equiangular drives and pulsed QSP sequences, optimized against
noise-matched figures of merit, achieve $\sigma^2_\nu$ up to $3$--$4\times$ lower
than two-level Ramsey spectroscopy under white and $1/f$ noise.
The smoothness of the drive envelope is shown analytically to govern the
high-frequency roll-off of the filter function, connecting pulse-shaping choices
directly to noise rejection in specific spectral bands.
Global Phase Spectroscopy is identified as near-optimal for band-limited high-pass
noise through a spectral concentration mechanism, and pulsed QSP sequences are
shown to recover the full sensitivity of two-level Ramsey in the three-level system.
All results are validated by exact filter-function calculations and Monte Carlo
simulations, with analytical conditions for the validity of the linear-response
approximation derived and verified numerically.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================

Optical atomic clocks have reached unprecedented levels of precision, with systematic
uncertainties at or below the $10^{-18}$ level \cite{Bloom2014,Ushijima2015,McGrew2018}.
Yet further improvements---particularly for portable and fieldable clocks---require confronting
two practical challenges that are not fully addressed by the standard two-level Ramsey protocol:
(i) maximizing the \emph{DC sensitivity} of the atomic response so that small frequency
deviations produce large, resolvable signal changes, and
(ii) suppressing \emph{fast frequency noise} (laser phase noise, magnetic field fluctuations,
vibrations) that falls within the bandwidth of the interrogation sequence.
Both challenges are ultimately questions about how a quantum system processes and filters
its environment, and both can be addressed through careful design of the pulse sequence
applied to the atomic transition.

Composite pulse sequences have a long history of improving measurements beyond the
standard Ramsey protocol, with techniques originating in NMR spectroscopy subsequently
adapted to precision atomic clocks.
Hyper-Ramsey sequences~\cite{Yudin2010,Huntemann2012} and their extensions cancel
systematic light-shift errors by combining multiple free-precession intervals with
tailored pulse phases, and have been demonstrated at the $10^{-18}$ level in
optical lattice clocks.
Dynamical decoupling techniques---CPMG and its variants---suppress low-frequency
dephasing in qubits and trapped ions by interleaving refocusing $\pi$ pulses, with the
noise-filtering effect quantified via the \emph{filter function}~\cite{Cywinski2008,Green2013}:
the spectral overlap of this function with the noise power spectral density directly
yields the shot-to-shot variance.
Walsh-function modulated sequences extend this approach to multi-tone noise
environments~\cite{Hayes2011,Magesan2011}, and filter-function-tailored sequences have
been experimentally demonstrated in superconducting qubits and trapped
ions~\cite{Biercuk2009,Bylander2011}.
Global Phase Spectroscopy (GPS)~\cite{GPS} takes a complementary approach: rather than
pulsed refocusing, it encodes the laser detuning as a global phase accumulated during
a cyclic SU(2) evolution, reading out via a long-lived clock state in a three-level
$\Lambda$ system.
GPS shifts filter-function weight toward the Rabi frequency, offering potential
advantages in $1/f$ and band-limited noise environments while retaining DC sensitivity
at all Rabi orders.
Despite this progress, no unified analytical framework has existed to compare GPS, Ramsey,
and general composite-pulse protocols on equal footing, or to systematically optimize
sequences for a given noise environment.

Quantum Signal Processing (QSP)~\cite{LowChuang2017} provides a systematic algebraic
framework for designing pulse sequences that implement prescribed polynomial transformations
of quantum matrix elements~\cite{LowGMN2019,Gilyen2019}.
Although QSP was originally developed in the context of quantum algorithms, its
structure---alternating fixed \emph{signal} rotations encoding an unknown parameter
with programmable \emph{signal processing} phase shifts---maps directly onto the
composite pulse sequences used in atomic spectroscopy.
Extending this formalism to continuous-time noise processes---what we term
\emph{temporal QSP}---yields a systematic framework for characterizing and optimizing
the noise-filtering properties of clock protocols, unifying previously disparate
sequence families within a single algebraic structure.

The present work departs from prior art in three respects.

\textit{Multilevel clock systems.}
Existing filter-function analyses~\cite{Cywinski2008,Green2013,Soare2014} are developed
for two-level (qubit) systems; they treat control pulses as instantaneous and are
designed for superconducting qubits and trapped-ion spin-echo experiments rather than
optical clock architectures.
Three-level $\Lambda$ systems---in which a metastable state $|m\rangle$ serves as a
long-lived population reservoir---introduce a structurally distinct noise channel.
The laser detuning couples only the probe subspace $\{|g\rangle,|e\rangle\}$ via
$H_\delta = \delta|e\rangle\langle e|$ rather than acting symmetrically, and finite-duration
pulses accumulate noise over the full drive; both effects are absent in instantaneous-pulse
analyses.
Temporal QSP is developed from the outset for this three-level geometry, yielding
separate closed-form expressions for the probe and clock channels and a DC consistency
condition that is invisible in qubit analyses.

\textit{Clock-specific figure of merit.}
Prior filter-function work---including dynamical decoupling sequences (CPMG and its
variants~\cite{Cywinski2008,Biercuk2009,Bylander2011}) and Walsh-modulated sequences~\cite{Hayes2011,Magesan2011}---
optimizes noise suppression without constraint on signal sensitivity.
For quantum memory and decoherence suppression this is appropriate: the goal is to
keep a stored state intact, and signal readout plays no role.
For a clock, however, suppressing noise at the cost of signal sensitivity is
counterproductive: the Allan deviation is governed by the ratio of integrated noise
power passed by the filter function to the squared signal slope---a single figure of
merit, denoted $\sigma^2_\nu$, that depends on both noise rejection \emph{and} DC sensitivity simultaneously.
A DC consistency condition---derived here and shown to be a structural consequence of
the unitarity of the propagator---ties these two quantities together and rules out
sequences that suppress noise by sacrificing sensitivity.
The temporal QSP framework tracks both quantities jointly and optimizes $\sigma^2_\nu$
directly, a step that prior filter-function analyses do not take.

\textit{Analytical pulse shaping via temporal QSP.}
Composite-pulse and dynamical-decoupling analyses treat the drive envelope as a given;
envelope shaping has been studied primarily through numerical optimization.
Temporal QSP provides an analytical route: for GPS-type continuous drives with a
prescribed envelope family, the filter function's high-frequency roll-off exponent is
determined exactly by the boundary smoothness of the Rabi-frequency envelope.
We derive closed-form roll-off laws for square, raised-cosine, and Planck-taper
envelopes, connecting the algebraic order of the pulse edges directly to the clock's
out-of-band noise rejection.
This extends temporal QSP beyond pulsed sequences to analytically tractable shaped drives,
a direction not covered by prior QSP or filter-function work.

The GPS protocol~\cite{GPS} demonstrated that, under the correct laser-detuning
Hamiltonian (where only the excited state is energetically shifted), integer-cycle
resonance retains full DC sensitivity at all Rabi orders; prior claims of
filter-function nulls at the drive frequency arose from an incorrect symmetric
approximation to this Hamiltonian.
The temporal QSP framework resolves the questions left open by those results: what
optimality properties noise-optimized sequences possess, how envelope shaping governs
spectral roll-off, and whether pulsed sequences can surpass the DC sensitivity of all
continuous-drive three-level protocols.

In this work, we develop \emph{temporal QSP}: an extension of canonical QSP to
continuous-time noise processes that captures finite-pulse-duration effects and the
full spectral structure of the noise environment.
Temporal QSP provides a closed-form calculus for analyzing any given protocol and
a systematic framework for sequence optimization.
We address the following central questions in clock protocol design:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \emph{How can the noise-filtering properties of any clock protocol be characterized
        and compared on a common footing?}
        We derive a closed-form filter function for any pulse sequence on both two-level
        and three-level $\Lambda$-clock systems, expressed directly in terms of the
        amplitudes of the propagator.
        A key structural result---the DC consistency condition---ties the filter function
        at zero frequency to the squared signal slope, so sensitivity and noise
        suppression are not independently tunable.
        We use two-level Ramsey and its three-level double-$\pi$ analogue as
        reference protocols throughout.

  \item \emph{Which sequences minimize the normalized frequency variance, and how large
        are the gains?}
        Multi-segment equiangular sequences and pulsed QSP protocols, optimized under
        noise-matched figures of merit, substantially outperform two-level Ramsey:
        $N=16$ equiangular segments achieve $\sigma^2_\nu$ $3.5\times$ lower under white
        noise; optimized QSP sequences ($n=4,8,13$) achieve $3$--$4\times$ lower under
        both white and $1/f$ noise.
        GPS $m=1$ is near-optimal for high-pass band-limited environments, achieving
        $\sim60\times$ reduction relative to Ramsey when the noise spectrum lies below
        the drive frequency---an effect arising from spectral sparsity of the filter
        function rather than from asymptotic roll-off.

  \item \emph{What governs the high-frequency roll-off of the filter function?}
        For GPS and equiangular sequences, the filter function is dominated by a single
        spectral component whose high-frequency fall-off is controlled entirely by the
        boundary smoothness of the Rabi-frequency envelope: a square drive gives
        power-law roll-off $\propto\omega^{-4}$, a raised-cosine envelope gives
        $\propto\omega^{-12}$, and gradients over Fourier-parameterized envelopes can
        optimize the roll-off for a given noise model.

  \item \emph{Can a three-level clock match the sensitivity of two-level Ramsey?}
        Pulsed QSP sequences optimized over pulse angles and phases converge
        to a double-$\pi$ structure whose DC sensitivity matches that of optimal
        two-level Ramsey spectroscopy, recovering the $4\times$ sensitivity advantage
        over continuous-drive three-level protocols.

  \item \emph{When does the linear-response filter-function picture break down?}
        We derive analytical conditions on the noise amplitude and interrogation time
        below which the Kubo approximation is quantitatively reliable,
        and verify these conditions numerically for Ramsey and GPS protocols.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Clock Performance Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}
% ============================================================

This section defines the two key figures of merit that quantify atomic clock performance
and establishes the figure of merit (FOM) that combines them into a single optimization target.
The noise variance entering the FOM is expressed as a spectral overlap integral
between the noise power spectral density and a filter function that depends entirely
on the pulse sequence; this connection is made quantitative by the temporal QSP
framework developed in Sec.~\ref{sec:temporal_qsp}.

\subsection{Frequency sensitivity}
\label{sec:freq_sensitivity}

A clock operates by measuring an observable $M$ that depends on the laser-atom detuning
$\delta$, and feeding the result back to correct the local oscillator.
The quality of a single measurement is captured by the \emph{per-shot frequency sensitivity}:
\be
  \sigma^2_\nu(\delta) \equiv \frac{\mathrm{Var}(M)}{|\partial_\delta \langle M \rangle|^2}\,,
  \label{eq:sigma_nu}
\ee
which is the variance of the measured signal divided by the square of the slope of the
mean signal with respect to detuning.
This has units of frequency$^2$: it is the minimum frequency deviation detectable
in a single shot, and it is what the clock servo directly minimizes.

On resonance ($\delta = 0$), the numerator has two contributions:
\be
  \mathrm{Var}(M) = \mathrm{Var}_{\rm QPN}(M) + \langle \delta M^2 \rangle_{\rm noise}\,,
  \label{eq:var_total}
\ee
where $\mathrm{Var}_{\rm QPN}(M)$ is the quantum projection noise (QPN)---the irreducible
variance of any binary quantum measurement on a single atom---and
$\langle \delta M^2 \rangle_{\rm noise}$ is the variance driven by technical noise in
the laser or control fields.
QPN sets a fundamental floor that scales as $1/N_{\rm atoms}$ and is reduced by using
more atoms or entangled states; it is independent of the pulse sequence and therefore
does not enter the protocol comparison performed here.
This paper focuses on technical-noise suppression---the pulse-sequence-dependent
contribution---which is the dominant limit in modern optical lattice clocks operating
at or near the QPN floor.
Good clock design means both \emph{maximizing the signal slope}
$\mathcal{S} = |\partial_\delta\langle M\rangle|_{\delta=0}$ and
\emph{minimizing the noise variance} $\langle\delta M^2\rangle_{\rm noise}$.

For wide-sense stationary technical noise, the shot-to-shot classical noise variance of the
signal mean $\langle M\rangle$ is
\be
  \overline{\langle \delta M \rangle^2}_{\rm noise} = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}
    \frac{d\omega}{2\pi}\, S_\beta(\omega)\, \mathcal{F}(\omega)\,,
  \label{eq:noise_var_general}
\ee
where $S_\beta(\omega)$ is the (two-sided) noise power spectral density and $\mathcal{F}(\omega)$
is the \emph{filter function} of the pulse sequence, derived in Sec.~\ref{sec:temporal_qsp}.
The filter function is entirely determined by the trajectory of the quantum state during
the pulse sequence: different protocols (Ramsey, GPS, CPMG, equiangular) differ only in
the shape of this trajectory.

\subsection{Figure of merit}
\label{sec:fom}

The normalised per-shot frequency variance combines both metrics into a single
figure of merit (FOM).
A key structural insight, which we call the \emph{DC consistency condition},
is that the filter function evaluated at zero frequency equals the squared signal slope:
\be
  \mathcal{F}_e(0) = \left|\int_0^T|G(t)|^2\,dt\right|^2 = \mathcal{S}^2
  = |\partial_\delta\langle M\rangle|^2_{\delta=0}\,.
  \label{eq:dc_consistency}
\ee
The filter function $\mathcal{F}_e(\omega)$ therefore describes the sequence's
frequency response at \emph{all} frequencies on a common scale: at $\omega=0$ it
equals the sensitivity squared (how well the sequence discriminates DC detuning),
and at $\omega > 0$ it describes how strongly the sequence couples to noise
oscillating at that frequency.

This observation suggests the natural bandlimited FOM
\be
  \sigma^2_\nu(S_\beta) \equiv
  \frac{\displaystyle\int_{\omega_{\min}}^{\infty}
    \frac{d\omega}{2\pi}\,S_\beta(\omega)\,\mathcal{F}_e(\omega)}
  {\mathcal{F}_e(0)}\,,
  \label{eq:fom}
\ee
where the lower cutoff $\omega_{\min} = 2\pi/T$ is the \emph{Fourier resolution
limit}: noise fluctuations slower than one full interrogation cycle of duration $T$
cannot be resolved by a single shot and contribute only a shot-independent bias,
not to the frequency estimation variance.
By the DC consistency condition~\eqref{eq:dc_consistency}, the denominator equals
$\mathcal{S}^2 = |\partial_\delta\langle M\rangle|^2$, so $\sigma^2_\nu$ is
precisely the per-shot frequency variance~\eqref{eq:sigma_nu} evaluated over
the resolvable frequency band.

The FOM has a transparent physical interpretation: the numerator is the
noise-weighted filter function integrated over all frequencies the clock can
actually resolve (above the Fourier limit), and the denominator is the DC filter
value that sets the clock's sensitivity to constant detuning.
For three-level $\Lambda$-clock protocols, the standard Ramsey sequence achieves
$\mathcal{F}_e^{\rm Ramsey}(0) = T^2/4$; any sequence that reduces $\mathcal{F}_e(0)$
below this value while also worsening noise performance is doubly penalized.
The fundamental upper bound is $\mathcal{F}_e(0) = T^2$,
achievable by the double-$\pi$ QSP construction (Sec.~\ref{sec:qsp_results}) and by
optimal two-level Ramsey spectroscopy~\cite{Ramsey1950}.

In practice, numerical optimization of $\sigma^2_\nu$ over sequence parameters
can encounter degenerate solutions in which $\mathcal{F}_e(0)\to 0$ (vanishing
sensitivity) trivially minimizes the ratio.
To avoid this, we minimize the Ramsey-normalized composite objective
\be
  f \equiv \frac{\mathcal{F}_e^{(R)}(0)}{\mathcal{F}_e(0)}
         + \frac{\mathcal{V}}{\mathcal{V}^{(R)}}\,,
  \label{eq:score_inv}
\ee
where $\mathcal{F}_e^{(R)}(0)$ and $\mathcal{V}^{(R)} =
\int_{\omega_{\min}}^\infty \mathcal{F}_e^{(R)}(\omega)S_\beta(\omega)\,d\omega/(2\pi)$
are the DC sensitivity and noise variance of the three-level Ramsey reference sequence
under the same noise model, and $\mathcal{V} = \int_{\omega_{\min}}^\infty
\mathcal{F}_e(\omega)S_\beta(\omega)\,d\omega/(2\pi)$.
Both terms equal unity at the Ramsey operating point (total $f = 2$), rendering the
objective dimensionless and independently interpretable: the first term rewards DC
sensitivity above Ramsey's, and the second rewards reduced noise coupling.
The objective is bounded below by zero and diverges whenever $\mathcal{F}_e(0) \to 0$,
preventing degenerate collapse to vanishing sensitivity.
Protocol comparisons throughout the paper report $\sigma^2_\nu$ as the primary
metric; $f$ is used as the numerical optimization objective in
Sec.~\ref{sec:analytics} and Supplemental~Sec.~SVI.

Minimizing $\sigma^2_\nu$ is equivalent to minimizing the per-shot frequency
estimation variance.
The FOM depends on the noise model: different noise spectra $S_\beta(\omega)$
favor different protocol designs.

\paragraph{Connection to Allan deviation.}
For a clock with per-shot interrogation time $T$ and atom cycle time $T_c \geq T$,
the fractional frequency instability averaged over $\tau = N T_c$ is
$\sigma_y(\tau) \approx \nu_{\rm at}^{-1}\sqrt{\sigma^2_\nu/(N)} = \nu_{\rm at}^{-1}\sqrt{\sigma^2_\nu T_c/\tau}$,
where $\nu_{\rm at}$ is the atomic transition frequency.
Thus $\sigma^2_\nu$ sets the pre-factor of the Allan deviation directly:
protocols with lower $\sigma^2_\nu$ produce a lower Allan floor at every averaging
time $\tau$.
This connection assumes that different shots are statistically independent, which
holds when the noise correlation time is shorter than $T_c$.
The breakdown of linear response and the appearance of fringe-hopping at large
$\epsilon_{\rm rms}$ degrade the Allan deviation beyond the predictions of
$\sigma^2_\nu$; these non-perturbative effects are discussed in
Sec.~\ref{sec:strong_noise}.

Throughout this paper we compare protocols under three noise models of practical importance:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \emph{White noise}: $S(\omega) = S_0$, modeling short-timescale LO instability.
  \item \emph{$1/f$ noise}: $S(\omega) = S_0/|\omega|$, modeling low-frequency flicker noise.
  \item \emph{High-pass noise}: $S(\omega) = S_0\,\Theta(|\omega| - \omega_c)$ with
        $\omega_c = 2$\,rad/s, modeling a noise floor that exists only above a $1/f$ corner
        frequency.
\end{enumerate}
For $1/f^\alpha$ noise environments, the noise power is concentrated at low frequencies and
falls with increasing $\omega$.  The overlap integral~\eqref{eq:noise_var_general} is then
minimized by making $\mathcal{F}_e(\omega)$ small at low frequencies.
For high-pass environments, a protocol whose filter function is concentrated \emph{below}
the cutoff $\omega_c$ achieves nearly perfect noise rejection.
The relationship between FOM and the Allan deviation $\sigma_y(\tau)$, and the
breakdown of linear response at large noise amplitudes, are discussed in
Sec.~\ref{sec:strong_noise}.

\section{Temporal QSP and Kubo Linear Response}
\label{sec:temporal_qsp}
% ============================================================

This section develops the system-agnostic mathematical framework used throughout the paper.
We define the temporal QSP propagator and its amplitudes $F(t)$, $G(t)$,
derive the Kubo linear-response formula for the noise-driven variance in terms of two
scalar path functions built from this trajectory, and establish the general form of the
filter function $\mathcal{F}(\omega)$.
The specialization to specific physical systems---two-level Ramsey and three-level
$\Lambda$-clock---is deferred to Secs.~\ref{sec:two_level}--\ref{sec:three_level},
where the initial state, observable, and Hamiltonian structure are treated explicitly.
The filter function derived here applies equally to both system types;
its evaluation for each protocol class is presented in Sec.~\ref{sec:analytics}.

\subsection{The temporal QSP formalism}
\label{sec:tqsp_formalism}

A general pulse sequence consists of $d$ segments, each a continuous drive at Rabi frequency
$\Omega_j$ and phase $\phi_j$ for duration $\tau_j$ (total time $T = \sum_j\tau_j$).
Written in the $2\times 2$ SU(2) representation of the probe subspace
$\{|g\rangle,|e\rangle\}$, the propagator at time $t$ during segment $j$ is:
\begin{align}
  U_{\rm QSP}(t) &\equiv \begin{pmatrix} F(t) & iG(t) \\ iG^*(t) & F^*(t) \end{pmatrix}
  \label{eq:u_qsp_poly}
\end{align}
where the amplitudes $F(t), G(t)$ satisfy $|F|^2+|G|^2=1$ and obey the
recurrence (see Supplemental Material, Sec.~SX)
\begin{align}
  F_j(t) &= \cos\tfrac{\theta_j(t)}{2}\,f_{j-1} - e^{-i\phi_j}\sin\tfrac{\theta_j(t)}{2}\,g_{j-1}^*\,,
  \label{eq:recurrence_F} \\
  G_j(t) &= \cos\tfrac{\theta_j(t)}{2}\,g_{j-1} + e^{-i\phi_j}\sin\tfrac{\theta_j(t)}{2}\,f_{j-1}^*\,,
  \label{eq:recurrence_G}
\end{align}
with $\theta_j(t) = \Omega_j(t - t_{j-1})$, $f_0 = 1$, $g_0 = 0$.

The global amplitudes $F(t)$, $G(t)$ are assembled piecewise from these
per-segment solutions.  Defining segment boundaries $t_j = \sum_{k=1}^j\tau_k$
(with $t_0=0$, $t_d=T$), the Heaviside step function $\Theta$ selects the active segment
at each time $t$:
\begin{align}
  F(t) &= \sum_{j=1}^{d} F_j(t-t_{j-1})\,\bigl[\Theta(t-t_{j-1})-\Theta(t-t_j)\bigr]\,,
  \label{eq:F_piecewise}\\
  G(t) &= \sum_{j=1}^{d} G_j(t-t_{j-1})\,\bigl[\Theta(t-t_{j-1})-\Theta(t-t_j)\bigr]\,,
  \label{eq:G_piecewise}
\end{align}
where each $F_j(\cdot)$, $G_j(\cdot)$ satisfies~\eqref{eq:recurrence_F}--\eqref{eq:recurrence_G}
with initial conditions $(f_{j-1},\,g_{j-1})$ at the start of segment $j$.
The Fourier integrals~\eqref{eq:Phi}--\eqref{eq:Chi} then decompose into a sum of
$d$ segment contributions, each evaluated over $[t_{j-1},t_j]$.

The within-segment behavior of $F_j(t)$, $G_j(t)$ depends on the protocol class
and gives rise to two qualitatively different recurrences:
\begin{description}
  \item[Equiangular] Continuous drives ($\Omega_j\neq 0$) give
        $\theta_j(t)=\Omega_j(t-t_{j-1})$ growing linearly in $t$, so $F_j$, $G_j$
        trace smooth arcs on the SU(2) manifold throughout each segment.
  \item[Pulsed QSP] Free-evolution segments have $\Omega_j=0$, so $\theta_j(t)=0$
        throughout.  Equations~\eqref{eq:recurrence_F}--\eqref{eq:recurrence_G}
        then reduce to $F_j(t)=f_{j-1}$ and $G_j(t)=g_{j-1}$ (constant):
        the amplitudes remain frozen between instantaneous rotation pulses.
\end{description}

Two scalar path functions encode the noise-filtering properties:
\be
  \phi(t) = F^*(t)G(t)\,,\qquad \chi(t) = |G(t)|^2\,,
  \label{eq:path_functions}
\ee
and their Fourier transforms:
\begin{align}
  \Phi(\omega) &= \int_0^T F^*(t)G(t)\,e^{-i\omega t}\,dt\,,
  \label{eq:Phi} \\
  \mathcal{X}(\omega) &= \int_0^T |G(t)|^2\,e^{-i\omega t}\,dt\,.
  \label{eq:Chi}
\end{align}
The shape of the path $t\mapsto (F(t),G(t))$ on the SU(2) manifold---not just its endpoint---determines
the filter function.

\subsubsection{Pulsed QSP protocol}
\label{sec:qsp_pulsed}

In canonical QSP~\cite{LowChuang2017,LowGMN2019}, a unitary sequence alternates a fixed \emph{signal operator}
$W(\theta)$ with classically controlled \emph{signal processing operators}
$e^{i\phi_j\sigma_\varphi}$:
\be
  U_{\rm QSP} = e^{i\phi_0\sigma_\varphi}
    \prod_{j=1}^{n} R_{\bb{n}}(\theta)\,e^{i\phi_j\sigma_\varphi}\,,
  \label{eq:canonical_qsp}
\ee
where $R_{\bb{n}}(\theta) = e^{i(\theta/2)\,\bb{n}\cdot\boldsymbol{\sigma}}$ is the
\emph{signal operator} encoding the (generally unknown) rotation angle $\theta$, and
$e^{i\phi_j\sigma_\varphi}$ are the \emph{signal processing operators} with
classically controlled phases $\{\phi_j\}$.  In the atomic-clock context the signal
is the phase accumulated during free evolution of duration $\tau_{\rm free}$ under a
detuned Hamiltonian, so the signal operator takes the form
$R_{\bb{z}}(\Delta\tau_{\rm free}) = e^{-i\Delta\tau_{\rm free}\sigma_z/2}$,
encoding the laser detuning $\Delta$ as the unknown signal angle $\theta = \Delta\tau_{\rm free}$.

The pulsed QSP sequences used in this paper adopt this signal--processing structure
but generalize the processing operators to $xy$-plane rotations
$R(\theta_j,\phi_j) = e^{-i(\theta_j/2)(\cos\phi_j\,\sigma_x+\sin\phi_j\,\sigma_y)}$
in which \emph{both} the rotation angle $\theta_j$ and the axis angle $\phi_j$ are
optimized.  The full unitary for $n$ pulses separated by $n-1$ equal free-evolution
gaps is:
\be
  U_{\rm pulsed} =
    R(\theta_n,\phi_n)\,W\,R(\theta_{n-1},\phi_{n-1})\,W\cdots W\,R(\theta_1,\phi_1)\,,
  \label{eq:qsp_pulsed}
\ee
where $W = e^{-i\Delta\tau_{\rm free}\sigma_z/2}$ is the signal operator and
$\tau_{\rm free} = (T - \sum_j\tau_j)/(n-1)$ with fast pulse durations
$\tau_j = \theta_j/\Omega_{\rm fast}$ ($\Omega_{\rm fast} = 20\pi$) short compared to
$\tau_{\rm free}$.  This extends canonical QSP---where $\theta$ is fixed and only the
processing phases $\{\phi_j\}$ are free---to a family parameterized by the $2n-1$
independent parameters $\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_n\in(0,\pi]$ and
$\phi_2,\ldots,\phi_n\in[0,2\pi)$ ($\phi_1=0$ by gauge).
Ramsey is recovered as the special case $n=2$, $\theta_j = \pi/2$, $\phi_j = 0$.

Because the free-evolution segments satisfy $F_j(t)=f_{j-1}$ and $G_j(t)=g_{j-1}$ (constant),
the path functions $\phi(t)=F^*G$ and $\chi(t)=|G|^2$ are piecewise constant between pulses.
Each instantaneous rotation steps them to a new constant value via the SU(2)
recurrence~\eqref{eq:recurrence_F}--\eqref{eq:recurrence_G} at zero duration.
The Fourier integrals~\eqref{eq:Phi}--\eqref{eq:Chi} therefore reduce to a sum of
sinc-like terms, one per free-evolution gap.

These sequences are optimized using the same procedure as equiangular (see Supplemental Material, Sec.~SVI).
The double-$\pi$ sequence ($n=2$, $\theta=[\pi,\pi]$, $\phi=[0,0]$) maximizes DC
sensitivity; optimized sequences for $n=4,8,13$ use the Ramsey-normalized FOM to
minimize $\sigma^2_\nu$ instead.
Performance is compared in Sec.~\ref{sec:analytics}.

\subsection{Kubo linear response and the filter function}
\label{sec:kubo}

We compute the classical noise variance of the signal mean using the
toggling-frame linear-response formula.
The relevant quantity is the shot-to-shot variance
$\overline{\langle\delta M\rangle^2}_{\rm noise}$,
not the full quantum Kubo variance $\langle\delta M^2\rangle$.
For a QSP sequence described by amplitudes $F(t)$, $G(t)$ and a noise
Hamiltonian $H_\epsilon = \beta(t)\,\hat{A}$ coupling to the probe transition,
the classical noise variance takes the form
(full derivation in Supplemental Material, Sec.~SII):
\be
  \overline{\langle \delta M \rangle^2}_{\rm noise}
  = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{d\omega}{2\pi}\,
    S_\beta(\omega)\,\mathcal{F}(\omega)\,,
  \label{eq:kubo_general}
\ee
where $S_\beta(\omega)$ is the two-sided power spectral density of the noise
and $\mathcal{F}(\omega)$ is the \emph{filter function} of the pulse sequence.
The filter function is entirely determined by the first-order sensitivity trajectory
$r(t) = \langle\psi_0|[M_I(T), \tilde{A}_e(t)]|\psi_0\rangle$
through $\mathcal{F}(\omega) = |\mathcal{F}[r(t)](\omega)|^2$,
where $M_I(T) \equiv U^\dagger(T)\,M\,U(T)$ is the lab-frame observable conjugated
by the \emph{full} propagator at the end of the sequence.
A key property is the DC consistency condition
$\mathcal{F}(0) = \mathcal{S}^2$ (the filter function at zero frequency equals
the signal slope squared), ensuring the filter function and sensitivity
are not independent quantities.

For sequences satisfying $G(T)=0$ (Ramsey, GPS-$m$, equiangular), $M_I(T)$ is
proportional to $M = \sigma_y^{gm}$ in the $\{g,m\}$ subspace with
$M_I(T) = m_y M$, $m_y = (-1)^m$ for GPS-$m$
(Supplemental Material, Eqs.~\eqref{S-eq:MI_bloch}--\eqref{S-eq:r_G0}).
In this case $r(t) = i\,m_y\,|G(t)|^2$ and, since $m_y^2=1$:
\be
  \mathcal{F}(\omega) = |\mathcal{X}(\omega)|^2
    = \left|\int_0^T |G(t)|^2 e^{-i\omega t}\,dt\right|^2\,.
  \label{eq:Fe_general}
\ee
For general pulsed QSP sequences with $G(T)\neq 0$, $M_I(T)$ acquires
coherence-channel contributions and the filter function receives additional
cross-terms involving $\Phi(\omega) = \mathcal{F}[F^*G]$
(Supplemental Material, Eq.~\eqref{S-eq:r_general}).
For the two-level sensor (Supplemental Material, Sec.~SVIII), $r(t)\propto\mathrm{Im}[F^*G]$
and $\mathcal{F}(\omega) = |\Phi(\omega)|^2$; the population channel carries no
independent information in that case because $|G|^2 = 1-|F|^2$ is determined by
unitarity and the measurement of $\sigma_z$.
All $\Phi(\omega)$ and $\mathcal{X}(\omega)$ are entirely determined by the
noiseless trajectory, so different protocol classes differ \emph{only} in the
shape of $\mathcal{F}(\omega)$.
The explicit filter functions for each protocol are evaluated in
Sec.~\ref{sec:analytics}.

\section{Two-Level Spectroscopy}
\label{sec:two_level}
% ============================================================

This section specializes the temporal QSP framework to the two-level atom,
establishes the filter function and signal slope for Ramsey spectroscopy,
and sets the baseline against which three-level protocols are compared in
Sec.~\ref{sec:three_level}.

\subsection{Filter function}
\label{sec:two_level_ff}

For a two-level atom with probe states $\{|g\rangle,|e\rangle\}$, the optimal
observable for Ramsey spectroscopy is $M = \sigma_y$ measured on the equatorial
initial state $|\psi_0\rangle = (|g\rangle + |e\rangle)/\sqrt{2}$.
Under this choice the general filter function~\eqref{eq:Fe_general}
reduces to $\alpha=1$, $|\mathcal{X}|^2=0$, giving
\be
  \mathcal{F}^{(2L)}(\omega) = |\Phi(\omega)|^2
  \label{eq:Fe_2L}
\ee
(derived in Supplemental Material, Sec.~SVIII).
The population channel $|\mathcal{X}|^2$ vanishes for the following reason.
In a two-level system, $|F(t)|^2 + |G(t)|^2 = 1$ by unitarity, so
$|G(t)|^2 = 1 - |F(t)|^2$ is not an independent degree of freedom.
The observable $M = \sigma_y$ on the equatorial initial state evaluates to
$2\,\mathrm{Im}(F^*G)$ on any state reachable from $|\psi_0\rangle$, so its
noise response is captured entirely by the coherence channel $F^*G$;
the population channel carries no additional information and drops out of
the variance calculation.
Without a third reference state $|m\rangle$ that is unaffected by the drive,
there is no way to independently resolve $|G|^2$ from the measurement of $M$.

\subsection{Ramsey protocol: filter function and signal slope}
\label{sec:ramsey}

The standard Ramsey protocol consists of two $\pi/2$ pulses of duration
$\tau_p = \pi/(2\Omega_{\rm fast})$ separated by free precession of duration
$\tau_{\rm free} = T - 2\tau_p$.
In the fast-pulse limit $\Omega_{\rm fast} T \gg 1$, the two $\pi/2$ pulses are
nearly instantaneous and place the Bloch vector in the equatorial plane, after
which it precesses freely for duration $\tau_{\rm free}\approx T$.
During this free-evolution interval the drive is off ($\Omega=0$), so the
recurrences~\eqref{eq:recurrence_F}--\eqref{eq:recurrence_G} give constant amplitudes
$F(t)\approx f_1$ and $G(t)\approx g_1$, with $|f_1|^2+|g_1|^2=1$ and
$|g_1|^2\approx\tfrac{1}{2}$ after the first $\pi/2$ pulse.
Substituting into~\eqref{eq:Fe_2L} and integrating, the dominant contribution
from the free-evolution interval gives the analytic filter function
\be
  \mathcal{F}^{(2L)}_{\rm Ramsey}(\omega) = \frac{4\sin^2(\omega T/2)}{\omega^2}\,,
  \label{eq:F_ramsey}
\ee
with high-frequency roll-off $\mathcal{F}\sim\omega^{-2}$.
The signal slope for this optimal measurement is $\mathcal{S}_{\rm Ramsey} = T$,
consistent with the DC consistency condition $\mathcal{F}^{(2L)}_{\rm Ramsey}(0) = \mathcal{S}^2$:
\be
  |\mathcal{S}_{\rm Ramsey}|^2 = T^2 = 4\pi^2
  \quad \text{for } T=2\pi\,.
  \label{eq:ramsey_sens}
\ee
The noise integral under white noise gives the normalised frequency variance
$\sigma^2_{\nu,w} = \mathcal{V}_w/\mathcal{S}^2 \approx 8.1\times10^{-3}$
for $T=2\pi$, which serves as the primary baseline for all protocol comparisons in
Sec.~\ref{sec:analytics}.
Note that the commonly quoted $M{=}\sigma_z$ convention operating at the mid-fringe
($\delta = \pi/(2T)$) yields the same filter function~\eqref{eq:F_ramsey} and thus
the same $\sigma^2_\nu$; the $\sigma_y$ convention merely makes the DC working point
transparent.

\section{Three-Level Spectroscopy}
\label{sec:three_level}
% ============================================================

This section specializes the temporal QSP framework to the three-level
$\Lambda$ system used in Global Phase Spectroscopy (GPS).
We derive the three-level propagator, establish the filter function and
signal slope, prove that all GPS protocols share the same DC sensitivity
as Ramsey, and analyze the GPS filter function structure.

\subsection{Three-level $\Lambda$-clock propagator}
\label{sec:3level_propagator}

For the three-level $\Lambda$ system with states $|g\rangle$, $|e\rangle$,
$|m\rangle$, the physically correct laser-detuning Hamiltonian is
\be
  H_\delta = \delta\,|e\rangle\langle e|\,,
  \label{eq:H_delta}
\ee
which shifts only the optically excited state.
Working in the reference frame co-rotating with the probe laser, with both ground states
$|g\rangle$ and $|m\rangle$ at zero energy, the physical observable
$\langle\sigma_y^{gm}\rangle$ measures only the \emph{relative} phase between
$|g\rangle$ and the dark state $|m\rangle$.
Under $H_\delta = \delta|e\rangle\langle e|$, the excited state is displaced but
neither ground state acquires a phase, so the relative $|g\rangle$--$|m\rangle$ phase
is accumulated entirely through the drive-mediated coupling to $|e\rangle$.
Under a continuous drive at Rabi frequency $\Omega$ and detuning $\delta$ for
duration $\tau$, the three-level propagator is
\be
  U(\tau) = e^{-i\delta\tau/2}
  \begin{pmatrix}
      F(\tau) & iG(\tau) & 0 \\
      iG^*(\tau) & F^*(\tau) & 0 \\
      0 & 0 & e^{+i\delta\tau/2}
  \end{pmatrix},
  \label{eq:propagator_3L}
\ee
where the upper-left $2\times 2$ block is exactly the probe-subspace propagator
$U_{\rm QSP}(\tau)$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:u_qsp_poly}, with $F(\tau)$, $G(\tau)$
the amplitudes satisfying the recurrences~\eqref{eq:recurrence_F}--\eqref{eq:recurrence_G}
under the \emph{traceless} part of $H_\delta + H_{\rm drive}$.
The additional factor $e^{-i\delta\tau/2}$ is a global probe phase that
accumulates relative to the dark state $|m\rangle$ across each segment and
must be tracked cumulatively for multi-segment sequences; it has no analog
in the two-level case and is the origin of the correct GPS signal formula
derived in Sec.~\ref{sec:gps}.

\subsection{Filter function and signal slope}
\label{sec:three_level_kubo}

For the three-level clock, the initial state is
$|\Psi_0\rangle=(|g\rangle+|m\rangle)/\sqrt{2}$ and the observable is
$M = \sigma_y^{gm} = -i|g\rangle\langle m|+i|m\rangle\langle g|$.
We consider probe-laser frequency noise described by the Hamiltonian
$H_e = \beta_e(t)|e\rangle\langle e|$, which shifts only the optically excited state.
The classical noise variance~\eqref{eq:kubo_general} then reads
(derived in Supplemental Material, Sec.~SII):
\be
  \overline{\langle\delta M\rangle^2}_{\rm noise} = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{d\omega}{2\pi}
    \,S_{\beta_e}(\omega)\,\mathcal{F}_e(\omega)\,,
  \label{eq:main_result}
\ee
with the probe-noise filter function
\be
  \boxed{\mathcal{F}_e(\omega) = |\mathcal{X}(\omega)|^2\,,
  \qquad \mathcal{X}(\omega) = \int_0^T|G(t)|^2\,e^{-i\omega t}\,dt\,.}
  \label{eq:Fe}
\ee
The filter function depends only on the population channel $\chi(t)=|G(t)|^2$;
the coherence-channel contribution $|\Phi|^2$ from $F^*G$ drives quantum fluctuations
in the off-diagonal density matrix but does not contribute to the variance of the
classical signal mean $\langle M\rangle$ (see Supplemental Material, Sec.~SII.3).
We restrict attention to probe noise throughout; noise on the clock transition
$|m\rangle\langle m|$ (which produces a protocol-independent filter function) is
discussed in Supplemental Material, Sec.~SII.5.

Figure~\ref{fig:two_vs_three} illustrates $\mathcal{F}_e(\omega)/T^2$ for the
two-level and three-level Ramsey and GPS references alongside the double-$\pi$ QSP,
establishing the DC-sensitivity hierarchy that motivates the full protocol comparison
of Sec.~\ref{sec:filter_functions_comparison}.

The signal slope $\mathcal{S}=\partial_\delta\langle M\rangle|_{\delta=0}$
receives contributions from both the global probe phase and the detuning
dependence of the amplitudes in~\eqref{eq:propagator_3L}.
The analytic formula for GPS protocols is derived in Supplemental Material,
Sec.~SI; for general sequences $\mathcal{S}$ is evaluated numerically.

\subsection{GPS protocol: signal and DC sensitivity}
\label{sec:gps}

Global Phase Spectroscopy (GPS) \cite{GPS} drives the $|g\rangle
\leftrightarrow|e\rangle$ optical transition while reading out via the
long-lived clock transition $M=\sigma_y^{gm}$.
The GPS-$m$ sequence is a single continuous resonant drive at Rabi frequency
$\Omega=2\pi m/T$ for total time $T$, completing exactly $m$ Rabi cycles.

With the noise Hamiltonian $H_\delta=\delta|e\rangle\langle e|$, the
three-level clock signal is (see Supplemental Material, Sec.~SI):
\begin{align}
  S(\delta) &= \sin\!\left(\frac{\delta T}{2}\right)
               \cos\!\left(\frac{\Omega_R T}{2}\right)
  \nonumber \\
            &\quad
             -\frac{\delta}{\Omega_R}
              \cos\!\left(\frac{\delta T}{2}\right)
              \sin\!\left(\frac{\Omega_R T}{2}\right)\,,
  \label{eq:gps_signal}
\end{align}
where $\Omega_R=\sqrt{\Omega^2+\delta^2}$.
This differs from the probe-subspace-traceless formula; the two agree only
at $\delta=0$ where both vanish.
All protocols operate at $\delta=0$ (the quadrature point).

Differentiating~\eqref{eq:gps_signal} and evaluating at $\delta=0$
(where $\Omega_R\to\Omega$):
\be
  \partial_\delta S\big|_{\delta=0}
  = \frac{T\,\Omega\,\cos(\Omega T/2) - 2\sin(\Omega T/2)}{2\Omega}\,.
  \label{eq:gps_deriv}
\ee
For GPS-$m$ with $\Omega T=2\pi m$, $\sin(m\pi)=0$ and
$\cos(m\pi)=(-1)^m$, so
\be
  \partial_\delta S\big|_{\delta=0} = \frac{(-1)^m T}{2}\,,
  \qquad
  |\partial_\delta S|^2 = \frac{T^2}{4} = \pi^2
  \quad\forall\,m\,.
  \label{eq:gps_sens_result}
\ee
\emph{All GPS protocols at $\delta=0$ share the same DC sensitivity as
three-level $\pi/2$ Ramsey}: $|\mathcal{S}|^2 = T^2/4$ [Eq.~\eqref{eq:gps_sens_result}].
Note that this three-level DC sensitivity is four times below the optimal two-level $\pi/2$
Ramsey sensitivity $|\mathcal{S}|^2 = T^2$ (Sec.~\ref{sec:ramsey});
the double-$\pi$ Ramsey-type QSP construction of Sec.~\ref{sec:qsp_results} recovers this factor.

\subsection{GPS filter function structure}
\label{sec:gps_ff}

At $\delta=0$, the amplitudes for a GPS-$m$ drive evolve as
$F(t)=\cos(\Omega t/2)$, $G(t)=-i\sin(\Omega t/2)$, giving path functions
$\phi(t)=F^*G=-\tfrac{i}{2}\sin(\Omega t)$ and
$\chi(t)=|G|^2=\tfrac{1}{2}[1-\cos(\Omega t)]$.
Their Fourier transforms are computed from the standard integrals
$\int_0^T \sin(\Omega t)\,e^{-i\omega t}\,dt$ and
$\int_0^T [1-\cos(\Omega t)]\,e^{-i\omega t}\,dt$, giving
\begin{align}
  |\Phi(\omega)|^2 &= \frac{T^2}{4}\,\mathrm{sinc}^2\!\!\left(\frac{(\omega-\Omega)T}{2}\right)
    + \frac{T^2}{4}\,\mathrm{sinc}^2\!\!\left(\frac{(\omega+\Omega)T}{2}\right)\,,
    \label{eq:Phi_GPS} \\
  |\mathcal{X}(\omega)|^2 &= \frac{T^2}{4}\left[\mathrm{sinc}^2\!\!\left(\frac{\omega T}{2}\right)
    + \mathrm{sinc}^2\!\!\left(\frac{(\omega-\Omega)T}{2}\right)\right]\,,
    \label{eq:X_GPS}
\end{align}
where $\mathrm{sinc}(x)\equiv\sin(x)/x$.
The coherence-channel term $|\Phi|^2$ is peaked at $\omega=\pm\Omega$ and falls
as $(\omega\mp\Omega)^{-2}$ away from those peaks;
$|\mathcal{X}|^2$ has peaks at $\omega=0$ and $\omega=\Omega$.
The filter function $\mathcal{F}_e = |\mathcal{X}|^2$ therefore has a dominant
\emph{peak} at the Rabi frequency $\omega=\Omega$, with additional weight near $\omega=0$.
This is a key clarification: previous analyses using the probe-subspace-traceless
Hamiltonian predicted nulls at Rabi harmonics; these nulls disappear under the
correct Hamiltonian~\eqref{eq:H_delta} because the population channel $|\mathcal{X}|^2$
places weight precisely at $\omega=\Omega$.
Consequently, GPS-$m$ is \emph{most sensitive} to noise near $\omega\approx\Omega$.

The practical consequence for $\sigma^2_\nu$ is immediate (see Table~\ref{tab:fom}).
Under white noise (bandlimited FOM, $\omega\geq 2\pi/T$), GPS-$m$ has
$\sigma^2_{\nu,w}\approx 9.8\times10^{-3}$ ($m=1$) and $4.7\times10^{-2}$ ($m=8$),
both exceeding Ramsey's $8.1\times10^{-3}$, showing that GPS couples more strongly to white noise.
Under $1/f$ noise, GPS-$8$ is more competitive because the $1/f$
spectral weight at $\omega=8$ is eight times smaller than at $\omega=1$.
The full quantitative comparison across all protocols and noise models is
presented in Sec.~\ref{sec:analytics}.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Protocol classes}
\label{sec:protocol_classes}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Both optimizable protocol families below are instances of the temporal QSP
formalism (Sec.~\ref{sec:qsp}): they differ in whether the control is applied
as a continuous drive or as instantaneous pulses separated by free evolution.
They are evaluated alongside Ramsey and GPS as fixed analytic baselines.

\paragraph{Equiangular $N$-segment sequences.}
The equiangular protocol divides $T$ into $N$ equal segments of duration
$\tau = T/N$, each a continuous drive at a shared Rabi frequency $\Omega$ with
independently optimized phases $\phi_k$:
\be
  \prod_{k=1}^{N}
    e^{-i(\Omega\tau/2)(\cos\phi_k\,\sigma_x + \sin\phi_k\,\sigma_y)}\,.
  \label{eq:eq_seq}
\ee
GPS-$m$ is the degenerate case $N=1$ (a single uninterrupted drive).
The amplitudes $F(t)$ and $G(t)$ trace smooth arcs on the SU(2) manifold
throughout each segment.
The free parameters $(\Omega,\phi_2,\ldots,\phi_N)$ are optimized for a given
noise model using differential evolution followed by L-BFGS-B polishing
(Supplemental Material, Sec.~SVI).
For $N=4$ optimized under white noise (Ramsey-normalized objective,
$\omega_{\min}=2\pi/T$), the optimum gives
$\mathcal{F}_e(0)=10.68$ and $\sigma^2_{\nu,w}=3.46\times10^{-3}$ ($2.2\times$
below Ramsey); $N=8$ and $N=16$ reach $\sigma^2_{\nu,w}=2.63\times10^{-3}$
and $2.18\times10^{-3}$ ($2.9\times$ and $3.5\times$ below Ramsey).

\paragraph{Pulsed QSP sequences.}
Pulsed QSP sequences consist of $n$ fast rotations
$R(\theta_j,\phi_j) = e^{-i(\theta_j/2)(\cos\phi_j\,\sigma_x+\sin\phi_j\,\sigma_y)}$
separated by $n-1$ equal free-evolution gaps of duration $\tau_{\rm free}$,
as defined in Sec.~\ref{sec:qsp_pulsed}.
The amplitudes $F(t)$ and $G(t)$ are piecewise constant between pulses and
stepped instantaneously at each rotation.
For $n=2$, the double-$\pi$ sequence $\theta=[\pi,\,\pi]$ is included as a
fixed high-DC-sensitivity reference.
The first $\pi$ pulse maps $|g\rangle\to|e\rangle$, placing the atom entirely
in the excited state; free evolution under $H_\delta=\delta|e\rangle\langle e|$
for $\tau_{\rm free}\approx T$ then accumulates a full phase $\delta T$---twice
the half-phase $\delta T/2$ of equatorial Ramsey spectroscopy---and the second
$\pi$ pulse closes the sequence.
This doubles the signal slope, giving
$\mathcal{F}_e(0)\approx T^2\approx 4\pi^2$, a $4\times$ improvement over both
three-level Ramsey and GPS, and matching the optimal two-level Ramsey value.
Because $\mathcal{V}$ and $\mathcal{F}_e(0)$ scale together, $\sigma^2_\nu$ is
comparable to Ramsey; the double-$\pi$ advantage lies instead in dynamic range
and robustness to detection noise ($\mathcal{F}_e(0)$ directly reduces the
per-shot frequency uncertainty from photon shot noise).
For $n=4,8,13$, the $2n-1$ free parameters
$(\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_n,\phi_2,\ldots,\phi_n)$ are optimized with the
Ramsey-normalized objective (Supplemental Material, Sec.~SVI), reshaping
the filter function to break the $\mathcal{V}/\mathcal{F}_e(0)$ cancellation
and achieving $\sigma^2_\nu$ 2--4$\times$ below Ramsey.

The results of Table~\ref{tab:fom} establish a quantitative hierarchy of clock
protocols that depends on the noise environment.
No single protocol is universally optimal; the appropriate choice must be
matched to the dominant noise mechanism.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{White noise.}
        Ordering (lower $\sigma^2_\nu$ is better):
        QSP $n{=}13$ $<$ Eq.\,$N{=}16$ $<$ QSP $n{=}8$ $<$ Eq.\,$N{=}8$
        $<$ Eq.\,$N{=}4$ $<$ QSP $n{=}4$ $<$ Ramsey $\approx$ QSP $n{=}2$
        $<$ GPS $m{=}1$ $\ll$ GPS $m{=}8$.
        Optimized equiangular and pulsed QSP sequences both achieve substantially
        lower white-noise $\sigma^2_\nu$ than Ramsey: Eq.\,$N{=}16$ gives
        $2.18\times10^{-3}$ ($3.5\times$ below Ramsey) and QSP $n{=}13$ gives
        $1.99\times10^{-3}$ ($3.9\times$ below Ramsey).
        Both GPS protocols are worse than Ramsey because the single continuous
        drive concentrates $\mathcal{F}_e$ near the Rabi harmonic, contributing
        heavily to the noise integral under a flat noise spectrum.
        The double-$\pi$ QSP ($n{=}2$) achieves $\sigma^2_\nu\approx7.6\times10^{-3}$,
        comparable to Ramsey; its primary advantage is the $4\times$ higher
        DC sensitivity $\mathcal{F}_e(0)\approx T^2$ (Sec.~\ref{sec:qsp_results}),
        which widens the dynamic range and reduces the impact of measurement noise.
  \item \textbf{$1/f$ noise.}
        Ordering (lower $\sigma^2_\nu$ is better):
        QSP $n{=}13$ $\approx$ QSP $n{=}8$ $<$ Eq.\,$N{=}16$ $<$ QSP $n{=}4$
        $\approx$ Eq.\,$N{=}8$ $<$ Eq.\,$N{=}4$ $<$ Ramsey $\approx$ QSP $n{=}2$
        $<$ GPS $m{=}1$ $<$ GPS $m{=}8$.
        Because $1/f$ noise concentrates power at low frequencies, protocols
        whose $\mathcal{F}_e$ is shifted to higher frequencies are advantaged.
        Pulsed QSP performs best for $1/f$ noise: $n{=}13$ and $n{=}8$ give
        $\sigma^2_{\nu,1/f}\approx9.6\times10^{-4}$ and $9.8\times10^{-4}$,
        roughly $3.7\times$ below Ramsey ($3.59\times10^{-3}$).
        Equiangular $N{=}16$ achieves $1.3\times10^{-3}$.
        GPS-$1$ ($8.3\times10^{-3}$) is worse than GPS-$8$ ($9.0\times10^{-3}$)
        for $1/f$ noise---reversed from the white-noise ordering---because GPS-$1$'s
        filter function overlaps heavily with the low-frequency $1/f$ spectral weight.
        The double-$\pi$ QSP ($n{=}2$) matches Ramsey at $\sigma^2_\nu\approx3.6\times10^{-3}$.
  \item \textbf{High-pass noise ($\omega_c=2$).}
        GPS-$1$ is near-optimal: its filter function is almost entirely contained
        below $\omega_c=2$, giving $\sigma^2_{\nu,\rm hp}\approx6\times10^{-5}$
        (roughly $60\times$ better than Ramsey).
        This suppression is not primarily a roll-off effect but a spectral-structure
        effect: for GPS $m=1$ (square, single segment), $|G(t)|^2=\sin^2(t/2)=
        (1-\cos t)/2$ has Fourier series content only at $k=0$ and $k=\pm 1$,
        so $\mathcal{X}(\omega)$ is extremely small for $\omega\gtrsim 2$
        (Sec.~\ref{sec:pulseshaping}).
        GPS-$8$ performs worst ($4.3\times10^{-2}$) because its filter function
        peaks far above the cutoff.
        Among the optimized protocols, equiangular sequences best suppress
        high-pass noise: Eq.\,$N{=}4$ gives $8.9\times10^{-5}$ (comparable to
        GPS-$1$), and $N{=}8,16$ give $3.3\times10^{-4}$ and $3.2\times10^{-4}$.
        Pulsed QSP $n{=}13$ achieves $6.2\times10^{-4}$ and $n{=}8$ achieves
        $1.3\times10^{-3}$.
        The double-$\pi$ QSP ($n{=}2$) and QSP $n{=}4$ are comparable to Ramsey
        ($\approx3.8$--$3.9\times10^{-3}$) for this noise model.
\end{enumerate}
These design principles are further illustrated by the detailed noise-model
analyses in Secs.~\ref{sec:hf_noise} and~\ref{sec:lorentzian_noise} below.

All filter functions are computed via FFT of the exact propagator over a time

The simulation code passes the following unit tests:

\section{Validity of Linear Response Theory}
\label{sec:validity}
% ============================================================

This section establishes when the Kubo formula~\eqref{eq:main_result}---a first-order
perturbative result---accurately describes the noise-driven variance.
The linear response approximation underlies all the FOM values in Table~\ref{tab:fom},
and it is important to know the conditions under which it applies.

\subsection{Perturbative condition}
\label{sec:pert_condition}

The Kubo linear response formula is a first-order expansion in the noise Hamiltonian
$H_\epsilon$.
For this expansion to be valid, the noise must be weak relative to the QSP control:
the dimensionless parameter governing the expansion is
\be
  \epsilon = \left\|\int_0^T dt\, H_\epsilon(t)\right\| = \left|\int_0^T \beta(t)\,dt\right| \ll 1\,.
  \label{eq:pert_param}
\ee
For a noise process with PSD $S_\beta(\omega)$, the root-mean-square value of this
integral is
\be
  \epsilon_{\rm rms} = \sqrt{\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{d\omega}{2\pi}\,
    S_\beta(\omega)\left|\frac{1-e^{-i\omega T}}{i\omega}\right|^2}\,.
  \label{eq:epsilon_rms}
\ee
The linear response approximation is self-consistent when $\epsilon_{\rm rms} \ll 1$.

\subsection{Regime boundaries for specific noise models}

For a noise PSD of power-law form $S_\beta(\omega) = S_0\,(\omega_{\rm ref}/\omega)^\alpha$
with a high-frequency cutoff $\omega_c$:
\begin{align}
  \alpha = 0 \;(\text{white}): & \quad \epsilon_{\rm rms}^2 \approx S_0 T\,, \\
  \alpha = 1 \;(1/f): & \quad \epsilon_{\rm rms}^2 \approx S_0\,\omega_{\rm ref}\,T^2 \ln(\omega_c T)\,, \\
  \alpha = 2 \;(1/f^2): & \quad \epsilon_{\rm rms}^2 \approx S_0\,\omega_{\rm ref}^2\,T^3\,.
\end{align}
The transition from linear to nonlinear behavior depends critically on the noise color
and on the total interrogation time $T$.
For short interrogation times or weak noise, linear response is appropriate;
for long sequences or strong low-frequency noise, nonlinear effects become important
and one must turn to the direct simulation approach of Sec.~\ref{sec:strong_noise}.

\subsection{Instantaneous-pulse limit and perturbative corrections}
\label{sec:corrections}

In the limit $\Omega_{\rm fast} \gg \beta_{\rm rms}$ and pulse durations
$\tau_p \ll 1/\omega_c$, noise accumulated during pulses is negligible and enters
only through the free-evolution phases
$\delta_j = \int_{t_{j-1}}^{t_j} \beta(t)\,dt$.
Expanding the resulting noisy propagator to first order in $\delta_j$ recovers the
Kubo formula; the leading second-order Magnus correction scales as
$\epsilon^2\mathcal{F}^{(2)}(\omega)$ where $\mathcal{F}^{(2)}$ involves convolutions
of $\phi(t)$, and is negligible for $\epsilon_{\rm rms} \ll 1$.

\subsection{Numerical verification}
\label{sec:kubo_numerical}

We verify the validity boundary numerically by comparing the exact variance to the
Kubo prediction under white noise $S_\beta(\omega) = S_0$ for four protocols spanning
the range of sequence complexity considered in this work.

\paragraph{Ramsey (instantaneous-pulse limit).}
In the limit $\Omega_{\rm fast} \to \infty$, both $\pi/2$ pulses are instantaneous
and the accumulated phase is $\Phi = \int_0^T \beta(t)\,dt \sim \mathcal{N}(0,S_0 T)$.
The signal is $M = -\tfrac{1}{2}\sin\Phi$, so its variance can be computed exactly
to all orders in $S_0$:
\be
  \mathrm{Var}[M]_{\rm exact}
    = \frac{1 - e^{-2S_0 T}}{8}\,.
  \label{eq:ramsey_exact_var}
\ee
Expanding to first order gives the Kubo prediction
$\mathrm{Var}[M]_{\rm Kubo} = S_0 T/4$, consistent with
$F_e(\omega) = 4\sin^2(\omega T/2)$ integrated over all frequencies.
The ratio $\mathrm{Var}_{\rm exact}/\mathrm{Var}_{\rm Kubo}$ equals $1$ at small
$\epsilon_{\rm rms}$ and decreases monotonically as Kubo overestimates the saturating
exact variance.

\paragraph{GPS $m=1$.}
For a single resonant x-drive ($\Omega = 1$, $\Omega T = 2\pi$), first-order
perturbation theory gives $\delta M \propto -\int_0^T \beta(t)\sin^2(\Omega t/2)\,dt$,
so the linear-response variance is
\be
  \mathrm{Var}[M]_{\rm Kubo}^{\rm GPS\,1}
    = S_0 \int_0^T \sin^4\!\bigl(\Omega t/2\bigr)\,dt
    = \frac{3\pi}{4}\,S_0\,.
  \label{eq:gps1_kubo}
\ee
We confirm this slope numerically via Monte Carlo propagation of the $2\times 2$
$g$-$e$ subspace with $3\times 10^4$ trajectories per noise level.

\paragraph{Equiangular $N=4$ and QSP $n=8$.}
The same Monte Carlo test is performed for an equiangular sequence with $N=4$ pulses
and a QSP sequence with $n=8$ pulses, both optimized for white noise (Table~\ref{tab:fom}).
These protocols involve multi-phase drives and larger sensitivity $S^2$, providing
a more demanding test of the filter-function Kubo formula.

Figure~\ref{fig:kubo_validity} shows the ratio of exact (or Monte Carlo) variance
to the linear-response Kubo prediction as a function of $\epsilon_{\rm rms} = \sqrt{S_0 T}$
for all four protocols.
All ratios track unity at small $\epsilon_{\rm rms}$, confirming that the
filter-function Kubo formula~\eqref{eq:kubo_general} holds across protocol types.
The Kubo formula is accurate to within $5\%$ for $\epsilon_{\rm rms} \lesssim 0.2$,
degrades to a $20\%$ overestimate near $\epsilon_{\rm rms} \approx 0.5$, and
overestimates by more than a factor of two for $\epsilon_{\rm rms} \gtrsim 1$.
The FOM values in Table~\ref{tab:fom} are therefore quantitatively reliable for
$\epsilon_{\rm rms} \ll 0.2$; beyond this point the direct simulation of
Sec.~\ref{sec:strong_noise} is required.

\section{Pulse Shaping for Improved Noise Suppression}
\label{sec:pulseshaping}
% ============================================================

For sequences satisfying $G(T)=0$---including GPS-$m$, Ramsey, and equiangular
protocols---the filter function reduces to (Sec.~\ref{sec:kubo})
\be
  \mathcal{F}_e(\omega) = |\mathcal{X}(\omega)|^2, \qquad
  \mathcal{X}(\omega) = \int_0^T |G(t)|^2\,e^{-i\omega t}\,dt\,.
  \label{eq:Fe_GPS}
\ee
Pulse shaping---smooth modulation of $\Omega(t)$---controls the high-frequency roll-off
of $\mathcal{F}_e$ by governing the boundary smoothness of $|G(t)|^2 = \sin^2(\theta(t)/2)$,
where $\theta(t)=\int_0^t\Omega(s)\,ds$ is the cumulative pulse area.

\subsection{Filter design via path-function engineering}
%\LZ{One comment I have to this section is that it would be useful to have a plot of sensitivity functions for GPS with different pulse shapes, and then also have another plot with the respective pulse shapes ($\Omega(t)$) for a fixed total interrogation time $t$. The roll-off of $\mathcal{F}_e(\omega) = |\mathcal{X}(\omega)|^2$
is governed by the boundary smoothness of $|G(t)|^2$: each additional vanishing derivative
of $|G(t)|^2$ at $t=0$ and $t=T$ contributes two extra powers of $\omega^{-1}$ to
$\mathcal{X}(\omega)$ and four extra powers to $\mathcal{F}_e$.
Table~\ref{tab:rolloff} summarises the predicted exponents, and
Fig.~\ref{fig:shaped_gps_ff} confirms them numerically for GPS $m{=}1$.

A key structural advantage of GPS-$m$ is that the integer-rotation condition
$\Theta = 2\pi m$ ensures $G(0)=G(T)=0$ for \emph{any} choice of envelope, because the
drive starts in the ground state ($g(0)=0$) and completes $m$ full Rabi cycles.
Consequently $|G(0)|^2=|G(T)|^2=0$ automatically, and moreover
$\partial_t|G|^2\big|_{t=0,T} = \sin(\theta)\,\Omega/2\big|_{t=0,T} = 0$ (since
$\sin(0)=\sin(2\pi m)=0$).
Thus even the square GPS envelope achieves $\mathcal{F}_e\sim\omega^{-4}$.

The roll-off of $\mathcal{F}_e(\omega) = |\mathcal{X}(\omega)|^2$ is governed by the
boundary smoothness of $|G(t)|^2$ via successive integration by parts.
By the Paley--Wiener theorem \cite{Katznelson2004}, if $|G(t)|^2$ has $n$ vanishing
derivatives at both $t=0$ and $t=T$, then
$|\mathcal{X}(\omega)| = O(\omega^{-n})$ and $\mathcal{F}_e \sim \omega^{-2n}$.

For GPS-$m$ as a single segment (no internal boundaries), the integer-rotation condition
pins $G(0)=G(T)=0$, so $|G(0)|^2=|G(T)|^2=0$ (one vanishing value) and
$\partial_t|G|^2\big|_{0,T}=\sin\theta\cdot\Omega/2\big|_{0,T}=0$ (one further vanishing
derivative, since $\sin(0)=\sin(2\pi m)=0$).
A square ($\Omega=\text{const}$) envelope therefore has
$\partial_t^2|G|^2\big|_0 = \Omega^2/2 \neq 0$, giving $n=2$ and
$\mathcal{F}_e\sim\omega^{-4}$ (Table~\ref{tab:rolloff}).
Smooth envelopes with $\Omega(0)=\Omega(T)=0$ make $\theta(t)\sim t^3$ near the boundaries
(since $\theta'=\Omega=0$ there), so $|G|^2 = \sin^2(\theta/2)\sim t^6$, giving $n=6$ and
$\mathcal{F}_e\sim\omega^{-12}$.
There is no algebraic upper bound: a $C^\infty$ envelope with all derivatives of $\Omega$
vanishing at the endpoints gives super-polynomial decay.


\subsection{Envelope optimization}
\label{sec:envelope_opt}

We parameterize the Rabi envelope as
\be
  \Omega(t) = \bar\Omega\!\left[1 - \sum_{k=1}^K b_k\cos\!\frac{2\pi k t}{T}\right],
  \label{eq:rabi_param}
\ee
with $\sum_k b_k = 1$ enforced to give $\Omega(0)=\Omega(T)=0$ (raised-cosine class,
$\mathcal{F}_e\sim\omega^{-12}$).
The Fourier coefficients $b_2,\ldots,b_{K+1}$ are optimized at fixed $\delta=0$
by minimizing the Ramsey-normalized FOM
\be
  \mathcal{J} = \frac{1}{\mathcal{S}^2}
    + \frac{\mathcal{V}[\text{seq}]}{\mathcal{V}[\text{Ramsey}]},
  \label{eq:rn_obj}
\ee
with $\omega_* = 2\pi/T$ (Fourier limit).
Under white noise the optimized envelope (Opt0 in Fig.~\ref{fig:shaped_gps_ff})
achieves $\sigma_\nu = 0.0055$, a $1.8\times$ improvement over the square GPS pulse
($\sigma_\nu = 0.0098$) and below the Ramsey value ($\sigma_\nu = 0.0075$).
The improvement arises because the smooth envelope suppresses
$\mathcal{F}_e$ at high frequencies without the sharp spectral features of the
Hann or Blackman windows, which over-suppress near-band content and worsen the
integrated noise.
Note that Hann, Blackman, and Opt0 all belong to the same $\omega^{-12}$ class,
yet Hann and Blackman perform worse than the square pulse under white noise,
confirming that roll-off exponent alone does not determine the FOM.

The Planck-taper envelope (Table~\ref{tab:rolloff}, $\varepsilon=0.2$) illustrates
the trade-off between spectral concentration and DC sensitivity.
Its flat center preserves a full Rabi rotation but the $C^\infty$ ramps reduce the
effective sensing time, giving $\mathcal{F}_e(0)=6.33$---about $36\%$ below Square---so
$\sigma^2_{\nu,w}=0.025$ under white noise, worse than both Square and Ramsey.
For bandlimited noise, however, the narrow passband is decisive:
$\sigma^2_{\nu,{\rm hp2}}=6\times10^{-4}$ under high-pass noise ($\omega_c=2$),
$6\times$ better than Ramsey ($3.8\times10^{-3}$) and only slightly above Square
($10^{-4}$).
The Planck-taper is thus a natural choice when the dominant noise lies above the
Rabi frequency and strong spectral isolation is required, at the cost of some
sensitivity in broadband environments.

\section{Strongly Noisy Regime: Direct Clock Simulations}
\label{sec:strong_noise}
% ============================================================

When $\epsilon_{\rm rms} \gtrsim 1$, linear response theory breaks down and one must
return to direct time-domain simulation to assess clock performance.
In this regime the concept of a single filter function is no longer meaningful;
instead, clock performance is characterized by the Allan variance computed from
many realizations of the noisy clock cycle.
Direct simulations incorporating the full clock servo are reserved for future work;
here we analyze one practically important nonlinear effect---fringe-hopping---that
can be treated analytically and that directly limits GPS performance at moderate noise.

\subsection{Dynamic range and fringe-hopping}
\label{sec:fringe_hop}

In the strongly noisy regime ($\epsilon_{\rm rms} \gtrsim 0.3$), GPS protocols are
additionally susceptible to fringe-hopping.
Under the correct detuning Hamiltonian $H_\delta = \delta|e\rangle\langle e|$,
the GPS-$m$ error signal at $\delta=0$ takes the form
$S(\delta) \approx (-1)^m\sin(\delta T/2)$
(derived in Supplemental Material, Sec.~SI).
The fringe half-width is therefore
$\delta_{\rm lock}^{\rm GPS} \approx \pi/T$---identical to Ramsey's
$\delta_{\rm lock}^{\rm Ramsey} \approx \pi/T$---so GPS and Ramsey have
the same dynamic range against fringe-hopping.
The servo locks to an adjacent fringe when $\sigma_\delta \gtrsim \pi/T$.
The per-cycle fringe-hopping probability is
\be
  P_{\rm hop} \approx \mathrm{erfc}\!\left(\frac{\pi/T}{\sqrt{2}\,\sigma_\delta}\right),
  \label{eq:phop}
\ee
where $\sigma_\delta = [\int_0^T S_\beta(\omega)\,d\omega/(2\pi)]^{1/2}$.
For GPS-$m=8$ under white noise at $\epsilon_{\rm rms} = 0.5$,
$P_{\rm hop} \approx 10^{-3}$; the crossover occurs near $\epsilon_{\rm rms} \approx 1$,
well outside the linear-response regime.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================

We have introduced temporal Quantum Signal Processing as a unified framework for
analyzing and optimizing atomic clock protocols under realistic noise environments.
The framework connects the algebraic structure of any pulse sequence---encoded in
the amplitudes $F(t)$ and $G(t)$---to the two key performance metrics:
the per-shot frequency sensitivity $\mathcal{S}^2$ and the noise filter function
$\mathcal{F}_e(\omega)$.
A DC consistency condition, $\mathcal{F}_e(0) = \mathcal{S}^2$, ties these two
quantities together and directly sets the pre-factor of the clock's Allan deviation.

The framework reveals a layered performance structure.
All continuous-drive three-level protocols (GPS, equiangular) share a common DC
sensitivity $\mathcal{S}^2 = T^2/4$, a factor of four below optimal two-level Ramsey;
performance differences among them are therefore determined entirely by filter-function shape.
The double-$\pi$ pulsed QSP reference ($n=2$) breaks this constraint, achieving
$\mathcal{F}_e(0)\approx T^2$ and closing the gap to two-level Ramsey.
The filter function for GPS and equiangular sequences is governed by the clock
channel $|\mathcal{X}(\omega)|^2$ alone (since $G(T)=0$); under the correct
detuning Hamiltonian $H_\delta = \delta|e\rangle\langle e|$, this channel is
peaked at the Rabi frequency rather than nulled there, as incorrectly predicted
by the traceless approximation.

The quantitative comparison of Table~\ref{tab:fom} establishes a noise-environment-dependent
protocol hierarchy; no single protocol is universally optimal.
Optimized pulsed QSP ($n=4,8,13$) and equiangular sequences ($N=8,16$) both achieve
$\sigma^2_\nu$ substantially below two-level Ramsey: 3--4$\times$ lower for white and
$1/f$ noise.
GPS $m=1$ is near-optimal for band-limited high-pass noise environments, with
$\sigma^2_\nu \approx 60\times$ below Ramsey when the noise cutoff exceeds the drive
frequency; equiangular $N=4$ matches GPS $m=1$ closely for this noise model.

The temporal QSP framework opens several natural directions for future work.
Gradient-descent optimization of Fourier-parameterized GPS envelopes yields $1.4\times$
lower white-noise $\sigma^2_\nu$ for $m=1$, confirming that the square GPS $m=1$
pulse is already near-optimal for high-pass noise with $\omega_c\geq 2\Omega$---a
consequence of the spectral sparsity of $|G(t)|^2=\sin^2(t/2)$---while leaving room
for shaped envelopes to improve white and $1/f$ noise performance.
Beyond pulse shaping, the extension to multi-axis noise (simultaneous dephasing and
amplitude noise), the incorporation of atom-light coherence time limits, and the
application to other multi-level quantum sensing platforms are all accessible within
the same algebraic structure of temporal QSP.
